\section{Introduction}

Einstein's Field Equations (EFE), formulated by Albert Einstein in 1915, are the cornerstone of general relativity and have revolutionized our understanding of gravitational phenomena. These equations describe how matter and energy influence the curvature of spacetime, thereby determining the motion of objects through gravitational interaction. The EFE establish a profound connection between the geometry of spacetime, represented by the \textit{Einstein tensor} $G_{\mu\nu}$, and the distribution of energy and momentum, encapsulated in the \textit{stress-energy tensor} $T_{\mu\nu}$.

Mathematically, the Einstein Field Equations are given by:
\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $G_{\mu\nu}$ is the Einstein tensor, encapsulating the curvature of spacetime.
    \item $\Lambda$ denotes the cosmological constant, introduced by Einstein to allow for a static universe.
    \item $g_{\mu\nu}$ is the metric tensor describing the geometry of spacetime.
    \item $G$ is the gravitational constant.
    \item $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum.
    \item $T_{\mu\nu}$ is the stress-energy tensor, representing the distribution of matter and energy.
\end{itemize}

Recent research has expanded the scope of the EFE beyond classical physics, aiming to bridge general relativity with quantum mechanics. This is manifested in efforts to develop \textit{quantum extensions} of Einstein's equations, where quantum informational measures such as \textit{entanglement entropy} are integrated to provide new insights into the structure of spacetime. These theoretical advancements seek to reconcile discrepancies between quantum theory and relativity, fostering a more comprehensive understanding of the universe.
